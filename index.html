About Me

Education: Currently I am a fourth-year undergraduate at Northeastern University majoring in physics, with minors in math and geology.

Research Interests: I am broadly interested in observational exoplanet astronomy, and enjoy a blended approach of both science and engineering. On the engineering side, I have worked on systems engineering to support exoplanet imaging missions, including the Orbiting Starshade and Orbiting Configurable Artificial Star (ORCAS) missions.

Testing Electronic Prototypes for the Compact Muon Solenoid (CMS)

Background:
The European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN) runs dozens of particle physics experiments, among them the Compact Muon Solenoid (CMS) experiment. CMS is one of the experiments served by the Large Hadron Collider (LHC), and is one of two detectors which presented the first results for the Higgs boson. I worked on the electromagnetic calorimeter (ECAL) detector, one of several layers of detectors present in CMS.
The LHC periodically goes through long shutdown periods of 2 years, during which time the luminosity (corresponding to the energy of the proton-proton collisions) of the beamline is upgraded. To keep up with increased luminosity and combat degradation of the detector components due to extreme radiation, the experiments along the LHC’s beamline are upgraded as well. Worn electronics and detector components are ripped out and either replaced or upgraded.

Description of work:
These new electronics need to be tested before they’re installed. This is where my work was focused: on developing a test environment for new electronics and calorimeters for the CMS ECAL. I worked with Dr. Giacomo Cucciati to develop H4DAQ, a data acquisition software which will be used at the H4 site along the SPS test beam. In this setup, particles arrive from the SPS test beam and pass through an alternating array of five hodoscopes and plastic scintillators, then through the test calorimeter, where the scintillation data is read by a VFE and FE array. Triggers from the hodoscopes and scintillators, warning signals from the SPS, and busy signals from the VFEs and FEs are fed into the trigger board, a CAEN DT 5495 multiple-FPGA unit, where an acquisition decision is made and passed to the run-control software.
During later iterations of this project, a VICE++ board was introduced for testing prototype VFE and FE boards. The full ECAL setup includes a data source (either photons from scintillation in a physical calorimeter, or simulated data), at least one full set of 5 VFE cards, at least one FE card per VFE set, and a complicated array of other electronics that are part of the data acquisition framework. The VICE++ allows the cards being tested to be decoupled from the rest of the DAQ framework and from each other, so that one can test any combination of VFE and FE cards (including if some, or all, of one type of card is missing).
